Projects

Although I have broad interests in science, technology (specifically artificial intelligence), and education, my current dissertation centers around the study of sleep. Traditionally, the sleep field assumed that the way the brain moves through the stages of sleep over the course of the night was a homogenous process. Meaning, if one area of the brain was in NREM sleep at one point in time, we would expect that all other areas of the brain would also be in NREM at the same time. We now know, however, that this is not the case. Different areas of the brain can be in different stages of sleep at the exact same time in humans and animals. In fact, some areas remain awake while others show signs of sleep at sleep onset. We have termed this asynchronous sleep. Below are the projects related to this topic that I am currently working on.

Asynchronous sleep in the mammalian brain (click to read our PNAS paper!)

Sex differences and changes in asychronous sleep across the estrous cycle in females (read Kevin Swift's paper on sex differences in sleep here!)

Effects of exposure to novelty and learning on asynchronous sleep (read my 2019 Society for Neuroscience abstract here!)
